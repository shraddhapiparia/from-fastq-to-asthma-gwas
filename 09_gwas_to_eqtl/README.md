# 09: GWAS to eQTL Interpretation

## Objective

Cross-reference top GWAS hits from module 06 against a tissue-specific eQTL reference to identify variants with potential regulatory consequences. Generate per-variant biological hypotheses by combining GWAS effect direction with eQTL effect direction, and produce a prioritized candidate table and summary figure for downstream functional follow-up.

---

## Why This Step Matters

GWAS identifies genomic positions associated with disease risk but says nothing about mechanism. The majority of GWAS hits fall in non-coding regions, making functional interpretation non-obvious. Overlapping a GWAS signal with an expression quantitative trait locus (eQTL) — a variant whose alleles are statistically associated with gene expression in a specific tissue — provides the first testable mechanistic hypothesis: the risk allele may influence disease by modulating the expression of a nearby gene in a disease-relevant tissue.

For asthma, tissue context is critical. Lung eQTLs reflect local airway biology — barrier function, smooth muscle regulation, inflammatory signalling — while whole blood and immune cell eQTLs capture systemic immune activation. A variant that is both a GWAS hit and a lung eQTL for a gene like ORMDL3 is a much stronger functional candidate than one with no eQTL evidence or an eQTL only in an unrelated tissue.

This module demonstrates the annotation-based prioritization step that precedes formal statistical colocalization in real genomics workflows.

## Workflow Overview

```
asthma_gwas.assoc.logistic (module 06)
    ↓
extract_top_gwas_hits.py          — filter to ADD test, sort by P, write top N hits
    ↓
map_gwas_to_eqtl.py               — left join hits onto eQTL reference by SNP ID
                                    generate interpretation column (OR direction × eQTL beta direction)
    ↓
plot_gwas_eqtl_summary.py         — horizontal bar chart of −log10(P), coloured by eQTL gene
    ↓
Prioritized candidate table       → biological hypothesis generation
```

The left join ensures all GWAS hits appear in the output regardless of eQTL match. A missing match is informative — it indicates the variant may act through a non-regulatory mechanism or requires an LD proxy search to find an eQTL at a nearby proxy variant.

## How to Interpret Results

### What does a GWAS hit + eQTL match mean?

A SNP appearing in both the GWAS results and the eQTL reference provides a testable hypothesis: the variant may influence disease risk by altering the expression of the eQTL target gene in that tissue. This is not proof of causality — it is the starting point for mechanistic investigation.

### Combining GWAS direction with eQTL direction

The `interpretation` column combines two pieces of information:

| GWAS OR | eQTL beta | Hypothesis |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| > 1 (risk allele) | > 0 (higher expression) | Risk allele increases expression of the target gene |
| > 1 (risk allele) | < 0 (lower expression) | Risk allele decreases expression of the target gene |
| < 1 (protective allele) | > 0 (higher expression) | Protective allele increases expression of the target gene |
| < 1 (protective allele) | < 0 (lower expression) | Protective allele decreases expression of the target gene |

**Direction consistency**: if the risk allele increases expression of a pro-inflammatory gene (e.g., IL13, FCER1A), the direction is biologically coherent with asthma pathophysiology. If the risk allele decreases expression of a barrier or regulatory gene, that is also biologically plausible. Neither guarantees causality.

**Direction inconsistency**: when GWAS and eQTL directions appear contradictory (e.g., risk allele decreasing an apparently protective gene's expression), this may reflect: (a) complex epistatic or tissue-specific effects; (b) the variant tagging a nearby causal variant through LD; or (c) the gene model being incomplete. These cases warrant follow-up, not dismissal.

### Tissue context for asthma

- **Lung**: directly relevant to airway biology. Lung eQTLs for ORMDL3, GSDMB, and CHI3L1 connect to epithelial barrier integrity, airway remodelling, and mucus production.
- **Whole blood**: reflects systemic immune activation. eQTLs for IL13 and FCER1A in blood capture IgE-mediated and Th2 inflammatory signalling, which are central to atopic asthma.
- A variant with eQTL evidence in both lung and blood for the same gene is a stronger functional candidate than one supported by a single tissue.

### Unmatched variants

Variants in `eqtl_annotation_candidates.tsv` with `NaN` in the eQTL columns either have no regulatory evidence in this reference or require LD-based proxy lookup to find overlapping signals. A missing match is not evidence of a non-functional variant.

## Decision Guidelines

| Observation | Action |
|-------------|--------|
| GWAS hit matched to eQTL in disease-relevant tissue | Prioritize for follow-up; direction consistency strengthens the hypothesis |
| GWAS hit matched to eQTL but in an unrelated tissue | Note as secondary candidate; tissue-specific functional evidence needed |
| GWAS hit with no eQTL match | Consider non-regulatory mechanisms (splicing, protein-coding, chromatin); consider LD proxy search |
| Opposite-direction GWAS OR and eQTL beta | Do not discard — may reflect indirect or tissue-specific effects; flag for deeper investigation |
| Multiple eQTL genes at the same locus | Common in gene-dense regions (e.g., ORMDL3/GSDMB/LRRC32 on 17q21); formal colocalization required to distinguish signals |
| No variants matched despite reference entries present | Check that GWAS SNP IDs match reference SNP IDs exactly; synthetic IDs from `plink --dummy` will only match if reference was built from the same run |

### Prioritization strategy

Rank candidates by:

1. **GWAS P-value** — lower P indicates stronger statistical evidence for association
2. **eQTL effect magnitude (|eqtl_beta|)** — larger effect sizes suggest stronger regulatory impact
3. **Tissue relevance** — lung > immune > blood for airway disease
4. **Direction coherence** — biologically plausible direction adds interpretive confidence

The top priority candidates are those with the lowest GWAS P-value, a large-magnitude eQTL in a disease-relevant tissue, and a directionally coherent effect.

## What This Affects Downstream

- **Biological follow-up**: prioritized candidates from `eqtl_annotation_prioritized.tsv` are the direct inputs for formal colocalization (COLOC, SuSiE), functional validation (CRISPR perturbation, reporter assays), and Mendelian randomization
- **Module 06 feedback**: eQTL annotation reveals which of the GWAS top hits have regulatory plausibility, informing which signals are worth fine-mapping
- **Multi-omic integration**: variants with both GWAS and eQTL evidence are natural entry points for integration with chromatin accessibility (ATAC-seq), histone modification (ChIP-seq), or proteomics data

---

## Key Concepts

**eQTL (expression quantitative trait locus)**: a genetic variant statistically associated with the expression level of a gene in a specific tissue. Trans-eQTLs act at a distance; cis-eQTLs (more common and better powered) act within ~1 Mb of the target gene.

**Annotation vs colocalization**: overlapping a GWAS SNP ID with an eQTL SNP ID is an annotation step — it identifies candidate pairs. Colocalization (COLOC, SuSiE-coloc) is a statistical test that asks whether the GWAS and eQTL signals at a locus are driven by the same causal variant. Annotation is a prerequisite for colocalization, not a substitute for it.

**Left join rationale**: all GWAS hits appear in the candidates table even without an eQTL match. This preserves visibility of unmatched variants, which may be functional through non-eQTL mechanisms or require a different reference to annotate.

**LD proxy limitation**: this module matches on exact SNP IDs. In real workflows, the GWAS lead SNP and the eQTL lead SNP may differ but be in high linkage disequilibrium (r² > 0.8). LD-aware lookup substantially increases the number of annotatable loci.

---

## Limitations

- **No colocalization**: shared SNP ID does not prove that the GWAS and eQTL signals are driven by the same causal variant. COLOC or SuSiE-coloc are required for that determination.
- **No LD proxy search**: only exact SNP ID matches are reported. Real workflows query LD proxies (r² > 0.8) to capture eQTL signals tagging the same haplotype.
- **Single tissue per SNP in reference**: the demo reference has one record per SNP. Real eQTL resources (GTEx, eQTL Catalogue) provide multi-tissue data; the same variant may be an eQTL in multiple tissues.
- **Synthetic SNP IDs**: the reference was built to match the `plink --dummy` output from module 05. Gene names are real; variant positions are illustrative.
- **Six-entry reference**: coverage is deliberately minimal. A genome-wide eQTL reference would annotate orders of magnitude more GWAS hits.

## Real-World Usage

In production post-GWAS workflows:

- **GTEx portal** (gtexportal.org) provides cis-eQTL data across 49 tissues for ~30,000 genes; query via the GTEx API or pre-computed summary statistics
- **eQTL Catalogue** (eqtlcatalogue.github.io) harmonizes eQTL data across >100 studies including immune cell types relevant to asthma (regulatory T cells, mast cells, basophils)
- **COLOC** and **SuSiE-coloc** test whether GWAS and eQTL signals share a causal variant; posterior probability of colocalization (PP.H4) > 0.8 is the standard threshold for declaring a colocalized signal
- **SMR** (summary-data-based Mendelian randomization) tests whether the eQTL gene is on the causal path to disease, providing effect estimates analogous to MR but using cis-eQTL instruments
- LD-aware lookup tools (eCAVIAR, PAINTOR) integrate LD structure to prioritize variants at colocalized loci
